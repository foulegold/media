## **What Are Cognitive Biases?**

Cognitive bias is a consistently pattern of deviation in judgment. Rather than analyze information in an objective way, human brains tend to use little shortcuts called heuristics. They facilitate speedy thinking yet can lead to warped conclusions by using heuristics.

When exposed to information dumps, however, individuals automatically take note of the things that they’re interested in, recent events or emotional happenings. Although these tendencies allow your brain to conserve cognitive resources, they can also cause it to misinterpret reality.

The human decision-making process is affected by cognitive biases in various factors; financial decisions, socio-relations, personal political opinions and risk assessment. They afflict people of all intelligence, educational attainment or professional sophistication.

The goal of understanding cognitive biases is not to try to eliminate them from one’s life—they are unlikely ever to be eliminated completely—but rather, understanding these theories provides greater awareness when these fallacies may be affecting judgment.

## **How Biases Develop in the Human Brain**

The human brain evolved in environments where rapid decisive action was more valuable than perfect accuracy. Early humans had to rapidly detect threats, identify allies and make survival decisions. This environment rewarded how to take mental shortcuts so the brain could process information.

Three primary factors create cognitive biases:

- **Information Overload** — Daily, the brain filters huge amounts of data.
- **The need for fast decision making** — Calculated analysis takes time and effort.
- **Emotional pull** — Emotions tend to drive what you see and remember.

Such pressures lead the brain to rely on simple patterns rather than detailed profiling. Thus, cognitive biases are not arbitrary mistakes, but rather predictable trends within the human mind.

## **5 Cognitive Biases That Undermine Our Thinking**

Researchers have documented dozens of cognitive biases, but a few crop up especially often in your day-to-day life. The table below provides a summary of some of the most impactful biases and their typical effects.

| Cognitive Bias | Description | Common Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Confirmation Bias | Tendency to seek information that supports existing beliefs | Reading only news that aligns with personal opinions |
| Anchoring Bias | Fixating too much on the first piece of information received | Assuming that the initial price that comes across your eyes was “correct” value |
| Availability Bias | The tendency to overestimate the likelihood of an event based on how easy it is to remember | After reading news articles about airplane crashes, we think these events are very common. |
| Overconfidence Bias | Believing one knows more or is more capable than they actually are | The belief that you can always predict your own outcomes reliably. |
| Bandwagon Effect | Holding onto beliefs just because many others are believed to hold them | Supporting a trend just because it is the rage |

These biases can challenge decision-making, often without the conscious awareness of those making the decisions. The first step to reducing their impact is realizing they exist.

## How To Make Better Decisions By Understanding Cognitive Bias

There are cognitive biases not only in academic studies or psychology experiments. They guide many of the everyday decisions people make.

### Personal Decision-Making

Individuals rely on mental heuristics when making decisions involving money, health or relationships. Confirmation bias, for example, can lead someone to ignore red flags about an investment when they are only taking in good information.

The availability bias also clouds people’s judgment about risks. Someone who has only just watched news coverage of a rare disease might now believe that it’s more prevalent than it actually is.

### Professional Environments

In the workplace, cognitive biases may influence hiring processes, performance appraisals and strategic planning. Managers may have unconscious biases in favor of candidates who look like them, or put too much stock in first impressions.

They can also create some anchoring bias in negotiations and financial forecasts, as the first number often unduly influences expectations without much rational basis for it.

### Social and Political Judgments

Cognitive biases have a profound effect on group behavior and public opinion. Sadly, people would believe this without questioning anything because of the bandwagon effect.

This cycle can contribute to the spread of misinformation, stave off social progress and allow collaboration loops where people are only made aware of views that share their position.

Understanding these mechanisms is crucial for having constructive conversations and making informed decisions.

## Why Bias Prevails — The Psychology of Biased Decision Making

One aspect of it that is particularly interesting is the persistence of cognitive biases. Even when people recognize these mental distortions, they still cause behavior.

The persistence pertains to the fact cognitive bias is easily sewn in the information-processor of our brains. They often operate automatically, even without conscious thought.

Many biases, moreover, are guided by emotional comfort. Confirmation bias, for example, allows us to hold on to whatever we already believe, and lends us avenues through which we might avoid the discomfort that comes with contradictory evidence.

There is also social reinforcement. As biases are held in a group so individuals gain confirmation of their views and these views become entrenched.

## The Voice of Cognitive Bias within You

Self-awareness, if we can build it, is a great antidote for minimizing the impact due to cognitive biases. And while they can never be eradicated entirely, one can also learn about the kinds of situations in which bias is most likely to take hold.

Here are a few red flags that might indicate cognitive bias:

- Logic is evaluated after the emotional response to information
- Instant acceptance of beliefs that conform to previous consensus
- Failing to assess the evidence
- Overconfidence in personal judgments
- Over-reliance on scarce and anecdotal evidence

Awareness of these signals encourages people to pause and reconsider how they are reasoning.

## Ways to Eliminate the Effects of Cognitive Biases

However, while cognitive biases are hardwired into human thought, there are many ways of reducing their impact.

The table below distills some useful methods for people to do in their daily lives.

| Strategy | Explanation | Action Step |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Seek Diverse Perspectives | Actively engage with ideas that oppose your beliefs | Expose yourself to different opinions through reading sources you disagree with |
| Let Decisions Marinate | Give a little space between judgment and decision | Short beets,  deciding not to make major purchases or change jobs  on impulse |
| Use Data and Evidence | Base decisions on reliable information rather than intuition | Compare statistics before forming conclusions |
| Seek Dissenting Opinions | Allow others to challenge your thinking | Talk to people outside of your social bubble |
| Look Back at Mistakes | Study past errors for patterns | Maintain a decision journal |

These approaches promote more careful thinking and help overcome mental shortcuts that come automatically.

## FAQs

### What is cognitive bias?

Cognitive bias is an inclination to think in a way that does not satisfy the rationality, and it affects how we react and judge people based on other judgments. Such cognitive bias is the result of mental shortcuts which enable urine to process data to make decisions quickly, but at the cost of making false judgments.

### When are cognitive biases ever useful?

Not necessarily. In complex situations, cognitive biases can help people make quick decisions. But they become a problem when those become persistent errors in reasoning or prevent people from taking in important information.

### Can Cognitive Biases Be Cured?

Not at all. Cognitive biases are aspects of the brain’s information-processing system. They can’t be “gotten rid of” altogether, but people can learn to become aware of them and reduce their power by critically analyzing the frame placed around facts.

### Why are cognitive biases so difficult to identify?

Cognitive biases happen automatically and often unconsciously. Because they seem like ordinary reasoning processes, people may be unaware that their thinking has been distorted.

### Cognitive Biases are not just for Experts and Professionals

Yes. Cognitive biases are blind: They hit us all, scientists and doctors and finance types alike. Expertise can dull some biases, but no one is bias-free.

### How to improve decision-making amid cognitive biases?

It pauses our thinking, draws in other perspectives, tests the evidence and scrutinizes choices for bias patterns.
