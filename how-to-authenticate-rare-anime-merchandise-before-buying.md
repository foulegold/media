## **Step 3: Look Over Packaging and Labels**

It’s often the case that at first glance the packaging holds a physical key to its legitimacy. Counterfeiters often skimp on any of these, where as proper companies make all the effort to insure that their product is presented well.

When examining packaging, check:

- *Logos / trade marks:**The official logos of the franchise, studio and manufacturer - Uniform and realistic printing, coherent presentation, correct placement.

- **Barcodes and product codes:** Verify whether the printed numbers match those found in reliable databases or the official site.

## **Step 4: Inspect the Build Quality andÂ Materials**

Genuine limited edition anime figures and collectibles tend to have great manufacture quality. A fake typically has shortcuts in materials and construction.

Focus on the following aspects:

- **Sculpting:** Look at how accurate the sculpt is to product pictures. Search for disparities in the facial structure, body size or tiny details.
- **Paint:** Clean paint, good shading and very little to no bleed for real ones. Sloppy or uneven paint job are signs of bad quality.
- **Quality of workmanship:** Realistic items are produced in sizes that range from 1:1 to 1:4 and made by using time tested methods and mold making techniques. Cheap, brittle or strongly chemical-smelling materials might be a sign of a fake.
- **Fit:** Loose joints, bad-fitting parts or evidence of glue are usually signs of low quality casts.
- **Weight and touch:** A material may feel particularly heavy if it is high-quality, whereas a low-weight or hollow feel is common in cut-rate copies.

While there is no one right answer for such matters, comparing an item in hand — or really high-resolution photos thereof — to any and all available official images is a great way to help find small but meaningful discrepancies.

## **Step 5: Check Serial Numbers and Certificates**

Many anime products such as high-end figures, art books, and collector’s boxes will have unique ID numbers.

Common authenticity indicators include:

- **Serial nos:** A few examples are numbered individually (“no. 248 / 1000” etc). All of these may be found on the base, on a certificate attached to the bottom or side of the product, packing material.
- **Authenticity certificates:** Legitimate certificates typically contain the franchise logo, publisher or manufacturer information and security features such as watermarks.
- **QR codes or online registration:** Some manufacturers offer the opportunity for buyers to register their items or verify codes on an official website.
- **Event / store limited marks** Those one-off items sold at events or in specific stores have stickers signifying them being a limited item.

You can always use serial number or certificate info and double-check it with the manufacturers system or documentation, customer service etc., if available.
