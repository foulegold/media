# TikTok vs Instagram vs Facebook: 2025 Engagement Rate Benchmarks Compared

Engagement rate is the number marketers reach for first when they compare platforms, and in 2025 the spread between TikTok, Instagram, and Facebook is wide enough that the platform alone redefines what a "good" result looks like. A typical by-follower benchmark for the year puts TikTok near 3.7%, Instagram around 0.5%, and Facebook close to 0.15% — TikTok earns roughly seven times the interaction rate of Instagram and more than twenty times that of Facebook on the same audience base. Teams checking their own posts against these norms often consult growth-focused resources such as [https://instasuccess.com.ua/](https://instasuccess.com.ua/) before deciding where to put their effort. The gap is not an artifact of one dataset. It traces back to how each platform decides who sees a post, and that decision is controlled by the recommendation algorithm rather than by the size of an account's following.

## How engagement rate is measured

Engagement rate is interactions divided by an audience base, multiplied by 100. The disagreement between reports — one source saying TikTok sits at 2.5%, another saying 4.6% — comes from which audience base sits in the denominator. Three formulas dominate, and each answers a different question:

- **By followers:** (likes + comments + shares + saves) ÷ followers. This formula rewards small accounts and punishes large ones, because a post that reaches a million non-followers still divides by the follower count alone.
- **By reach:** interactions ÷ accounts reached. This measures how compelling a post was to the people who actually saw it, with follower count removed from the equation.
- **By views:** interactions ÷ video views. Standard for short video, where a view costs almost nothing and the bar to provoke a like or comment is higher.

The same Instagram account can show 0.5% by followers and close to 10% by reach. Both numbers are correct; they describe different things. By-follower rates are the most widely published because follower count is public and comparable across accounts. By-reach rates are more honest about content quality but require analytics access. When you compare your performance to a headline benchmark, match the formula first. A by-reach result placed next to a by-follower benchmark will look inflated by an order of magnitude.

One more source of variation: the interaction set. Some reports count only likes and comments. Others add shares and saves, which favors Instagram (saves) and TikTok (shares). Year-over-year comparisons inside a single methodology are reliable. Cross-report comparisons of a single year are not.

## TikTok

TikTok's engagement rate climbed through 2025 even as the platform crossed a billion users, a trajectory that runs against the usual pattern where scale dilutes interaction. The driver is distribution. A new post is shown to a small test audience drawn from people who have interacted with similar content, regardless of whether they follow the account. If completion rate, rewatches, and shares clear the bar, the post is pushed to a larger pool. Follower count barely gates initial reach, so a 2,000-follower account and a 2-million-follower account start a video on comparable footing.

That mechanism produces two effects in the benchmark data. Small accounts post the highest rates — TikTok accounts under 100K followers have reached around 7.5%, while accounts above 10 million settle nearer 2.9%, because the by-follower formula divides by a larger denominator. And content type swings results hard. Educational and entertaining posts outperform promotional ones by a wide margin; a how-to or a genuine review attracts comments and shares that a product announcement does not.

Format discipline matters on TikTok in a way it does not elsewhere. Native vertical video edited inside the app, with on-screen text and a clear hook in the first two seconds, is the format the algorithm understands best. Reposted horizontal clips or watermarked exports from other platforms tend to underperform.

## Instagram

Instagram's by-follower engagement has drifted down for several years and held near 0.5% in 2025. The platform is a hybrid: Reels run through a recommendation engine similar to TikTok's, while the main feed and Stories lean on the social graph and lean toward the accounts a user already follows. That split shows up in the analytics. Reels can reach far beyond an account's followers and post engagement-by-reach figures several times higher than a static feed image, yet the by-follower average is dragged down by feed and carousel posts that circulate mostly among existing followers.

A specific 2025 signal worth tracking on Instagram: views rose while engagement rate slipped. More content is being served to more people, but a smaller share of them interact. For an Instagram account, reach and view counts often describe current performance more accurately than the headline engagement rate. Saves and shares — the two actions Instagram's ranking now weights heavily — are a better quality signal than likes, which have lost ranking influence.

Carousels remain Instagram's most reliable format for interaction per post, because each swipe is a chance to re-serve the post to a user who did not finish it the first time. Reels win on raw reach. Single images sit lowest on both counts.

## Facebook

Facebook's organic engagement rate sits around 0.15%, the lowest of the three by a wide margin, and the reason is structural rather than a content failure. The News Feed prioritizes posts from friends, family, and Groups over content from Pages. Organic Page reach has been throttled for years; a Page post typically reaches a small single-digit percentage of its followers without paid promotion. Divide a handful of interactions by a large follower base and the rate collapses.

The platform's own data shows a quiet recovery from earlier lows, helped by short video in the Reels surface and by Groups, where engagement is concentrated. Brands have responded by cutting Page posting volume sharply — average monthly Page output fell by roughly half over the year as teams reallocated effort. The practical read on Facebook in 2025: treat it as a paid distribution and retargeting channel, and as a home for community Groups, rather than as an organic content priority. Posts that do earn organic reach are usually short video or content that members share into Groups.

## Why recommendation algorithms create the gap

The benchmark gap is, at bottom, a distribution gap. The three platforms answer a different question when they decide who sees a post.

TikTok asks: who, anywhere on the platform, is likely to enjoy this? The For You page is an interest graph. It matches content to behavior signals — what a user finished, rewatched, shared, searched — and ignores follower relationships almost entirely for new content. A post's ceiling is set by how interesting it is, not by how many people already follow the account. High ceilings produce high engagement rates, because content that reaches the right strangers gets interactions from people who chose to keep watching.

Facebook asks a near-opposite question: which of this user's existing connections posted something worth showing? It is a social graph. Pages compete for a slice of feed real estate that is reserved mostly for personal connections, and they lose that competition by design. Low organic reach forces a low engagement rate.

Instagram sits between the two and splits the difference by surface. Reels behave like TikTok's interest graph. Feed and Stories behave like Facebook's social graph. The blended average lands between the extremes, which is exactly where the 2025 number falls.

A second mechanism widens the gap further. Interest-graph platforms create a self-reinforcing quality filter. Because reach is earned per post rather than inherited from follower count, weak posts die early and strong posts spread, so the content that does circulate is pre-selected for engagement. Social-graph feeds show a user their connections' posts whether those posts are compelling or not, which pulls the average rate down.

## How follower tier and industry shift the numbers

Headline averages hide most of the useful information. The same platform produces very different rates depending on account size, niche, and format. Smaller accounts almost always post higher by-follower rates, because their audience is concentrated and personally connected, and because the formula divides interactions by a smaller number.

| Platform | Smaller accounts | Larger accounts | Strongest organic format |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| TikTok | Up to ~7.5% (under 100K) | ~2.9% (over 10M) | Native vertical video |
| Instagram | Elevated for micro accounts | Lower at scale | Reels for reach, carousels for interaction |
| Facebook | Low across tiers | Very low | Short video and Group content |

Niche moves the line as much as size. On TikTok, education-focused accounts have reached above 7%, while agency and B2B-service accounts have sat below 1%. Food, beauty, and entertainment niches run hot because the content is visual and shareable; finance and professional services run cool because the audience interacts less even when reach is healthy. The lesson for benchmarking: compare yourself to your platform, your follower tier, and your industry at the same time. A 1.5% TikTok rate is poor for a beauty micro-account and strong for a B2B agency.

## A worked example: the same video on three platforms

Consider a small business with 8,000 followers on each platform that posts one 25-second product-demo video, vertical, with captions, on all three. The numbers below are realistic 2025 outcomes, not guarantees, and they show why a single engagement rate hides the real story.

1. **Post and let initial distribution run for 48 hours.** On TikTok, the video is shown to a test pool of a few hundred non-followers. Completion rate is strong, so it expands to 60,000 views — far past the 8,000 followers. On Instagram, the Reel reaches roughly 9,000 accounts, a mix of followers and a modest recommended push. On Facebook, the Page post reaches about 600 people, under 8% of followers.
2. **Count the interactions.** TikTok: 1,900 likes, 70 comments, 240 shares. Instagram: 280 likes, 18 comments, 30 saves. Facebook: 12 likes, 1 comment, 2 shares.
3. **Apply the by-follower formula** (interactions ÷ 8,000). TikTok lands near 27% — high because reach blew past the follower base. Instagram lands near 4%. Facebook lands near 0.2%.
4. **Apply the by-reach formula** (interactions ÷ reach). TikTok falls to about 3.7%, because the denominator is now 60,000. Instagram holds near 3.6%. Facebook rises to about 2.5%.
5. **Read the two views together.** By followers, TikTok looks twenty times better than Facebook. By reach, the three are within a few points of each other. The content was comparably engaging to the people who saw it. What differed was how many people each algorithm chose to show it to.

The exercise isolates the real variable. Facebook did not produce worse content engagement per viewer; it produced far fewer viewers. TikTok's headline advantage is mostly a reach advantage that the by-follower formula converts into an engagement-rate advantage. For planning, the by-reach numbers tell you where your content resonates, and the by-follower numbers tell you where the algorithm will hand you free distribution.

## What the gap does and does not tell you

A platform's benchmark is useful for setting expectations and useless as a verdict on content quality. A 0.15% Facebook rate does not mean a post failed; it reflects a feed that reserves space for personal connections. A 3.7% TikTok rate does not mean the content is excellent; it reflects an interest graph that hands new posts a real chance at reach.

Three practical cautions follow. Do not import a benchmark from one report and treat it as your target without checking its formula and interaction set. Do not compare a small account to an all-account average, since the by-follower formula structurally favors small accounts. And do not read engagement rate in isolation from reach — on Instagram especially, rising views with a flat rate is growth, not decline.

Engagement rate is also a poor proxy for business outcomes on its own. A TikTok with 240 shares and no link clicks may move less revenue than a low-engagement Facebook post that reaches a warm retargeting audience. Match the metric to the objective: shares and saves for awareness, comments for community, and downstream actions for conversion.

## FAQs

### Why is TikTok's engagement rate so much higher than Facebook's?

TikTok distributes posts through an interest graph that shows content to likely-interested strangers regardless of follower count, so reach is earned per post. Facebook's feed reserves most space for friends and family, throttling organic Page reach to a small share of followers. Higher reach to interested viewers produces a higher engagement rate; restricted reach produces a low one.

### Which platform should a small account focus on for organic growth in 2025?

TikTok offers the clearest path to reach beyond an existing audience, because follower count barely gates initial distribution. Instagram Reels offers a similar mechanism with a larger commerce-ready audience. Facebook rewards organic effort least and works better as a paid and Groups channel. A small account chasing organic reach should prioritize TikTok and Instagram Reels.

### Why do different reports give different engagement numbers for the same platform?

The numbers differ because of the formula and the interaction set. By-follower, by-reach, and by-view formulas can describe the same account with figures that vary by a factor of ten or more. Reports also differ in whether they count saves and shares, which industries they sample, and which post types they include. Compare within one methodology, not across reports.

### Is engagement rate by followers or by reach more accurate?

By-reach is the better measure of content quality, since it shows how the people who saw a post responded. By-follower is more comparable across accounts because follower count is public, which is why it appears in most published benchmarks. Use by-reach to judge your own content and by-follower to compare against external benchmarks.

### Does a high engagement rate guarantee more sales?

No. Engagement rate measures interaction, not conversion. A post with thousands of shares can drive fewer purchases than a lower-engagement post that reaches a warm audience ready to buy. Align the metric with the goal: awareness, community, or conversion are tracked by different signals.

### How much do follower count and industry change the benchmark?

Both change it considerably. Smaller accounts post higher by-follower rates than large ones on every platform, often by several multiples. Industry swings results further — visual, shareable niches like food and beauty run well above professional-services niches on the same platform. Always benchmark against your platform, follower tier, and industry together.

## Conclusion

The 2025 numbers are clear and consistent in their ranking: TikTok well ahead, Instagram in the middle, Facebook far behind, with the order holding across nearly every methodology. The size of the gap, though, is mostly a story about distribution. Interest-graph platforms hand new posts reach based on content signals, which lifts engagement rates; social-graph feeds reserve attention for personal connections, which suppresses them. Before you read your own results as success or failure, fix the formula you are using, compare against your own tier and industry, and read engagement alongside reach. The benchmark tells you what is normal for a platform. Your reach and your objective tell you whether a post actually worked.
